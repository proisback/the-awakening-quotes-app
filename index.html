Chapter one

Not a feed. A practice.

A feed gives you a thousand things to scroll past. Hitaarth gives you one thing to keep. Each day's idea arrives in three layers, and the fourth layer is tomorrow.

  1. The thought. A line worth rereading.
  2. The meaning. Why it matters, in plain words.
  3. The practice. One small action for today.
  4. Tomorrow. Another thought waits.

Read less. Carry more.

Chapter two

    A new thought is waiting. The practice compounds quietly.

In the app: swipe right to keep a thought, swipe left to write your own note on it.

Chapter three

What Hitaarth refuses to do.

Most apps are built to keep you inside. Hitaarth is built to send you back to your day.

  • No infinite feed. The library is finite and chosen, one idea at a time.
  • No engagement algorithm. Nothing decides what you see based on what kept you scrolling.
  • No ads. We don't sell your attention.
  • No account. We don't even know your name.

Hitaarth succeeds when it gives you something to carry into your day, not another reason to stay on your screen.

Chapter seven

Why it exists.

When our son was born, we named him Hitaarth: a Sanskrit word meaning one whose purpose is to do good.

Becoming a father changed what I noticed about my own days. Social media gave me endless information, but almost nothing that stayed. Every once in a while, though, a single sentence would change how I handled a conversation, a hard decision, an ordinary day.

I wanted a place where ideas weren't buried under algorithms. One meaningful thought at a time, with one small action attached.

That's how Hitaarth was born. It isn't named after my son as a tribute. It's named after the value I hope we both grow into.

If you leave a little calmer, a little kinder, or with a slightly better perspective than when you arrived, the app has lived up to its name.

Prateek Mehta

Chapter eight

Questions.

Is it free?

Yes. The whole library, all four languages, offline use, share cards: everything you see today is free. A small set of extra themes and fonts may become paid later; the daily practice stays free either way.

How is this different from a quotes app?

A quotes app hands you an endless pile to scroll. Hitaarth gives you one hand-picked thought a day, with a lesson and one small action attached, then sends you back to your day. The library is finite, chosen by hand, and nothing is decided by an algorithm.

Do I need an account?

No. There is nothing to sign up for. Your favorites, notes, and streak live on your device.

How do I install it without an app store?

Hitaarth is a web app. Open it in your browser, then choose "Add to Home Screen" (iPhone: Share button; Android: Install app in the browser menu). It opens like any other app afterward.

Does it work offline?

Yes. After your first visit, everything works without internet, including Hindi.

Is Hindi fully supported?

Yes. Every idea, lesson, and action exists in Hindi in Devanagari script, alongside English, Spanish, and French.

Where does my data live?

On your phone. Notes and favorites are stored locally and never uploaded. The only thing we count is anonymous usage numbers, with no cookies, and we skip even that if your browser asks not to be tracked.

Appendix

For curious builders.

Hitaarth is also a small product experiment, run in public by one person.

Every product reflects what its builder chooses to optimize for. These notes explain what I chose, and why.

Thesis: distribution before monetization. The share card is the growth engine, so the craft went there first.

Demand before rails.

The premium screen is a single "I want this" button, not a checkout. Payment infrastructure gets built if 20% of the first 50+ premium page visitors ask for it. Not before.

Measure without watching.

Analytics are cookieless and anonymous, honor Do Not Track, and never identify a person. The metrics doc is public.

Kill what can't ship.

An early premium promise (home-screen widgets) died when the platform couldn't deliver it. The copy was corrected everywhere the same day.
